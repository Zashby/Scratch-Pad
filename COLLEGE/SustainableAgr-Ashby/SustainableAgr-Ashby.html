The Future of Agriculture

The world population hit approximately 8 BILLION humans on Earth at the end of 2022 with an expectation of another almost 2 billion poeple by the end of the century. This means more resources to manage the various necessities of keeping a growing population alive. This means management of not only shelter, but also water and food resources which will be competing with the same space and transportation restrictions as everything else in modern society. For this reason, we need to rethink the way we tackle the everyday needs of society including the structure of our wasteful agricultural practices.

Recognizing and Eliminating Waste


Billions of pounds worth of agricultural products are disposed of at farms EVERY YEAR. Some of these losses are a result of environmental considerations, but a large portion occur due to a lack of business, making it more economical to just plow over instead of harvesting crops, or because of cosmetic defects which make the products be considered "unattractive". One of the first things we can do, as a society, is to recognize flaws in our societal norms and how we view food. This starts with being more responsible with our decisions regarding proper usage of purchased food, and our willingness to eat less with our eyes.


We can make our own personal difference by being more reasonable when purchasing perishable items during our weekly grocery trips. Take a look at how many groceries you have thrown out recently because of purchases which never get used before going bad, add up the total, and use it as a personal litmus test of how efficiently you are living your life. Do you throw away 10 dollars worth of food a week? Less? More? Food waste makes up the largest part of waste found in US landfills so you should not be terribly surprised. The first step is to acknowledge the individual's role in the problem before attempting to address it. By taking a more calculated approach to grocery shopping you can help prevent massive food waste.

Future of Agriculture


Another major issue of the growing need for food production relates to land usage, efficient water usage, produce reliability, and transportation considerations. These issues could be alieviated, though not likely resolved, by gardening at a local level. Having a home garden, urban gardening (or the usage of public "green spaces" to garden for a community) can be means of reducing the reliability on "big ag", and do your part to reduce the strain on our agricultural and transportation infrastructure.


Now, on the topic of sustainable agriculture at a large scale, this meets with the most resistance due to a lack of financial incentive to switch methods. However, some companies have switched over to newer methods of production which show promise. These can be small scale "enclosed loop" production which uses the grey water from fish to create nutrients to hydroponically grow local produce. This system efficiently uses its resources and is even able to produce consumable fish as well as the grown produce. Alternatively, the system can be a large scale "Vertical farm", which uses controlled climate conditions, and large vertical stacks to hydroponically grow crops. Both of these options can be situated in the heart of urban areas with little issue, and provide a local means of food production. Additionally, by being grown in a controlled environment the horticulturists in charge of production can better monitor and ensure the quality of the product.


The Choice is Yours

There are many options and approaches towards creating a more sustainable and efficient food distribution loop. The choices are ultimately left to the consumer and governing bodies to properly incentivize the appropriate usage and development of resources. The first step is merely to become educated on the issues, and draw your own personal conclusions on how to approach it. Thank you for reading!
